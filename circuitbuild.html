The Basics of Circuits

(back to home)

A circuit is any path through which electrons can flow.
Circuits are very useful for a multitude of purposes.
These are the basic parts of a circuit:

  • A power source: This provides electrons to power the circuit.

  • A wire: This allows the electrons from the power source to flow, creating a current.

  • A resistor: This resists the flow of electrons. Anything which the circuit provides power to (such as a lightbulb) is a resistor.

Parallel circuits are the other, more complex type of circuit. They differ in having multiple branches, and thus they are still able to function when a resistor goes out.

SAFETY

  • Handle the ends of an uninsulated wire with care. Use erasers or other insulated materials and indirectly touch the ends.
    (Note: this is not as big of a problem with insulated wire, but being careful with the wire's ends is very important.

  • The wire can get very hot.

  • Just use common sense in general.

The Procedure

Materials:

  • Battery

  • 4 segments of copper wire

  • 2 lights for use in circuit building

Part 1 of 2: Series Circuit

(for this circuit, use 3 of the 4 copper wire segments.)

The diagram:

Procedure:

  1. Carefully place one wire segment near each terminal of the battery.
    Don't connect them yet.

  2. Attach the other ends of these wires to each light.

  3. Attach the remaining wire to the lights so that they are connected together.
    By this point, all parts of the circuit except for the power source are in place.

  4. Now, touch the ends of the wires to the terminals of the battery. The lights should illuminate.

Now, try doing this:

Disconnect one of the lights from the circuit, or replace it with something nonconductive. Note that all of the others turn off.
If any of the lights were to break, this would also stop the flow of the rest of the circuit.
This is a disadvantage of series circuits. However, they are much simpler and easier to fix than the other type of circuit: parallel.

(also, note that one bulb is dimmer than the other, even if it is only slightly dimmer. this is because voltage drops across series circuits.)

Part 2 of 2: Parallel Circuit

(for this circuit, use all 4 copper wire segments.)

The diagram:

(as you can see, this circuit is a little more complex than the simple series circuit above.)

The Procedure:

  1. Place the ends of two of the wires near the battery, but don't connect them yet, as in step 1 of the series circuit procedure above.

  2. Connect the ends of these two wires to one of the lightbulbs. (if you were to give the circuit power at this point, it would be a working series circuit.

  3. Now, using the two remaining wires, connect this lightbulb to another lightbulb. This grants the electricity more than one path to follow, which is what makes this circuit parallel.

  4. Finally, connect the wires safely to the battery, and both lights should power on. Note that their brightness is equal, contrary to when the bulbs were connected in series.

Now, try doing this:

Disconnect one of the lights from the circuit like you did with the series circuit and keep the other one connected.
Notice how the other light doesn't go out. This is because, in a parallel circuit, if one current path cannot be used, there is still another one available for the electrons to flow through.
This is an advantage of a parallel circuit over a series circuit, and it is also used in house wiring plans, because different room power outlets can be turned on or off without affecting the rest of the building.
Parallel circuits do have a disadvantage in that they are more complex than series circuits and thus more difficult to repair and service.
